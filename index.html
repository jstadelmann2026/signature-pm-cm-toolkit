Step 2

Size up your project

Experienced teams right-size their approach: they match the level of planning, governance, and rigor to the size and complexity of the work. We group projects into three tiers, so you apply just enough structure and never more than the job needs. Knowing your tier also gives everyone a common language for the work.

Tier 1 · Small light touch

Well suited to a first-time lead, a coordinator, or anyone new to running projects
This sounds like you if
  • It mostly involves you and your close team
  • Fewer than about 25 people are affected
  • Only your manager is watching
  • Nothing major is being rebuilt
What you need
  • A short list of who is involved
  • A simple project plan
  • A quick weekly update
  • A closeout checklist at the end

Tier 2 · Medium standard

Best run by someone with a project or two behind them, or with a mentor alongside
This sounds like you if
  • A few teams or departments are involved
  • Roughly 25 to 500 people are affected
  • A director or senior leader is watching
  • Some ways of working will change
What you need
  • A full project plan and charter
  • A plan for keeping people informed
  • A risk list
  • A simple group that approves decisions

Tier 3 · Large major project

Standard practice is to lead or co-lead with an experienced project manager or the project office
This sounds like you if
  • It reaches across the whole organization
  • 500 or more people are affected
  • Top leadership is watching
  • Technology, process, and people all change
What you need
  • A senior sponsor and a steering group
  • Experienced project and change support
  • A clear plan with checkpoints
  • A way to track the benefits afterward

Roles and responsibilities

These jobs show up on almost every project. On a small one, the same person often wears several hats. That is fine, just make sure each job is covered.

ExpertsPeople who know the detail and help figure out how things should work

A simple way to share out the work

For each task, decide who actually does it, who signs off on it (just one person), who you should ask for input, and who simply needs to be kept in the loop. Project managers call this a RACI chart, but you do not need the jargon to use the idea.

Step 5

The project lifecycle

Almost every project, large or small, moves through these seven stages in order. You will not need every one on a small project, but it helps to see the whole path.

1
Get the go ahead
Agree what you are doing and why, and get it approved.
2
Plan it
Line up the team and make a simple plan.
3
Look into it
Understand how things work today.
4
Design it
Decide how things should work instead.
5
Build it
Make it and test it on a small scale first.
6
Launch it
Go live, and help people through the switch.
7
Check it worked
See if it delivered, and note what to do better.

What to do first, every time

  • Write down what you are doing and why, on one page. Get someone senior to agree to it.
  • List who is involved and who is affected.
  • Set a regular check-in so progress and problems surface early.
  • Use the templates in section 7 for each step. The questions in section 1 tell you which ones you need.
Step 6

Leading change

1Understand
What change management is, and the path you will follow

Projects deliver something new. Change management helps people actually use it. You can install a system or roll out a new process perfectly and still fall short if people do not adopt it. Change management is the work of bringing people along: helping them understand the change, get ready for it, and stick with it.

Good change follows the same path whether your project is small or large. These are the six moves. They line up with the five adoption stages (ADKAR) used elsewhere in this toolkit: the moves are what you do, the stages are what people experience.

Step 9 · Advanced toolkit

The advanced toolkit

For seasoned leads running medium and large projects. This is the deeper, click-through edition: classify the work, pick a governance model, walk the milestones, and pull the full template suite for every phase.

Classify the work, then run its playbook

Most initiatives are one dominant type. Your type sets the baseline deliverables and governance.

Technical / system

New or changed app, integration, platform, or infrastructure
Heavier discovery, a formal design, and a careful, tested cutover. Architecture review and a change advisory board approve the build and release.

Policy

A new or revised rule people must follow
Legal and risk review is a mandatory gate, and attestation and rollout matter as much as the wording.

Process

A redesigned set of steps, hand-offs, and controls
Map the current state, design the future state, and pilot before you scale. SOPs and training carry the change.

Org / team structure

A change to structure, reporting lines, or where work sits
Confidential stakeholder work, legal or works-council consultation, and careful sequencing of announcements.

Glossary and acronyms

Charter
The one document that authorizes a project and names its purpose, scope, sponsor, and budget.
Sponsor
The senior person who backs the project and owns its success.
RAID log
A running list of Risks, Assumptions, Issues, and Dependencies.
RACI
Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed. A chart of who does what on each task.
ADKAR
Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. A model for guiding people through change.
OCM
Organizational Change Management. The discipline of preparing and supporting people through a change.
UAT
User Acceptance Testing. Checking the build does what users actually need before go live.
PMO
Project Management Office. The central team that sets project standards and supports big efforts.
Stage gate
A checkpoint between stages where someone approves the project to continue.
